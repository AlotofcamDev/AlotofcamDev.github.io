Train Rush VR

Project Type: Immersive Game Environments Group Project
Date: 2025
Role: Programmer
Demo: Quest 2 Build
Train Rush VR is a project created for a VR class in my course. This project was made in a small team environment comprised of 6 people with different roles: 2 Programmers, 2 Artists, 1 Game Designer and a Music Student doing the music for the project. This was the first time the team had created a game for VR, but it was a good stepping stone for learning how to design games around the constraints of VR and Quest hardware.

Process Insights

Train Rush VR was developed as part of a VR-focused class project in a small collaborative team of six, consisting of two programmers, two artists, one game designer, and a music student. This was the first time the group worked within virtual reality, making the project both a challenge and a valuable stepping stone for learning how to design engaging experiences around the unique constraints of VR and the technical limitations of the Quest hardware.

The initial spark for the game stemmed from my own personal passion for steam trains. I wanted to capture the sense of excitement and nostalgia tied to these machines while framing the experience in a way that would feel dynamic in VR. The very first concept was inspired by the classic trolley problem thought experiment, where players would make split-second moral decisions about which track the train should follow. However, as the team explored the idea further, it became clear that while interesting conceptually, it didn’t lend itself well to the type of interactive and fast-paced gameplay we wanted to deliver in VR.

Through iteration, the concept shifted towards a defense-based shooter, reimagining the train not only as a vehicle but as the central stage of the action. Players are placed on the moving train and must defend it from incoming threats, creating a constant sense of momentum and urgency. This new approach aligned better with VR’s strengths—immersive environments, spatial awareness, and physical interaction—while still tying back to the original inspiration of trains as the heart of the experience.

Working in this cross-disciplinary team was essential to bringing the vision to life. The artists developed a stylized visual identity that both suited VR’s performance requirements and complemented the thematic focus. The programmers worked to optimize mechanics and ensure smooth interaction, while the music student’s score added an additional layer of tension and atmosphere, enhancing the feeling of riding through a hostile landscape.

Ultimately, Train Rush VR taught us not only how to design for the constraints of VR but also how to iterate on initial ideas to find what works best in practice. It was a formative project where inspiration, technical experimentation, and team collaboration came together to shape a playable and memorable experience.
