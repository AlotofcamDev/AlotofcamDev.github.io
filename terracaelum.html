Terras Caelum

Project Type: Interactive Narrative Design Group Project
Date: 2024
Role: Gameplay Designer
View: Document
Terras Caelum is a game design project where we had to create a TTRPG world using the Fate System as a base. This project was created over half a semester by a team of 6 people each with different roles for completing the document.

Process Insights

Designing Terras Caelum required adapting the flexibility of the Fate System to a world inspired as much by storytelling and worldbuilding as by mechanics. Fate’s focus on Aspects, Stunts, and collaborative narration provided a strong foundation. Still, to make the setting feel distinct, I leaned into archetypes, structured stunts, and environmental Aspects that ensured the world itself was an active force in play.

One of the earliest insights was the need to anchor player identity through archetypes. Fate excels at giving players freedom, but that freedom can feel overwhelming without guidance. Archetypes—such as Assassins, Musicians, and Crystal Engineers—gave players immediate thematic direction while still leaving space for personal Aspects. This choice lowered the barrier to entry for new players while still offering experienced ones meaningful creative expression.

Equally important was how Terras Caelum framed conflict and consequence. Rather than treating rolls as purely mechanical outcomes, I focused on pacing them as narrative beats, ensuring each contest or challenge shifted the story forward. Stunts were restructured as archetype-driven abilities rather than completely open-ended options, striking a balance between player creativity and system clarity. This reduced hesitation at the table and reinforced the world’s themes through mechanics.

The world itself was also a design inspiration, and here my influences became clear. The verticality and interconnected environments of Xenoblade Chronicles informed how I thought about Terras Caelum’s floating islands—not just as backdrops, but as narrative landscapes that shaped story opportunities. Similarly, the factional tensions and cultural identities of Final Fantasy XIV influenced how I designed the different societies—each with its own politics, values, and conflicts—so that encounters felt like part of a living world rather than isolated events. These inspirations encouraged me to treat the environment and its factions as narrative “players” alongside the characters.

Through playtesting, I learned that Fate’s open-endedness could sometimes intimidate newcomers. To mitigate this, I incorporated structured prompts and environmental cues. By letting the setting itself introduce obstacles or opportunities—such as resource scarcity, faction pressure, or a skyship on the horizon—players always had something to respond to. This encouraged improvisation without requiring players to invent everything themselves.

Looking back, the key lesson from this process was that designing within an existing system means balancing homage with innovation. Fate provided a framework for storytelling freedom, while Xenoblade Chronicles and Final Fantasy XIV offered models of expansive, character-driven worlds. By combining those influences, Terras Caelum became a system where archetypes, mechanics, and narrative all reinforce each other, allowing players to co-create stories of survival, identity, and conflict in a fractured world.
